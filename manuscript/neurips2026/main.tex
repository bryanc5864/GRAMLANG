\begin{abstract}
DNA foundation models---DNABERT-2, Nucleotide Transformer, HyenaDNA, Enformer, and PARM---have set the state of the art on regulatory sequence prediction, yet what they actually learn about \emph{cis}-regulatory grammar is unclear. We introduce \textbf{GRAMLANG}, a benchmark and diagnostic framework that tests whether these models encode motif arrangement (positions, orientations, spacing) on top of motif identity. Our central tool is the \textbf{Spacer-Factored Grammar Sensitivity Index (SF-GSI)}, which uses matched-spacer perturbations and a null-normalized null distribution to isolate arrangement effects from compositional confounds. Across five published massively parallel reporter assay (MPRA) datasets and five models (7{,}650 model--enhancer measurements), naive grammar-sensitivity tests flag \emph{every} enhancer as grammar-sensitive; once we factor out spacer composition and apply FDR correction, only $0.12\%$ ($9/7{,}650$) survive. Bootstrapped over enhancers, $89.7\%$ ($95\%$ CI: $87.7$--$91.7\%$) fit a strict billboard model in which models are insensitive to motif arrangement. Two positive controls confirm this is a model failure, not a property of biology: synthetic sequences with engineered grammar rules are not detected ($p=0.73$), and known $10.5$-bp helical periodicity is invisible to the embeddings ($p=0.96$). Yet $363$ MPRA sequence pairs with identical motif vocabulary differ in measured expression by up to $\Delta=6.3$ in $\log_2$-fold space---grammar matters biologically. The billboard model is therefore a description of \emph{model limitations}, not regulatory biology, and identifies a concrete capability gap for the next generation of DNA foundation models.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Foundation models pretrained on large genomic corpora---DNABERT-2 \citep{zhou2023dnabert2}, the Nucleotide Transformer (NT) \citep{dalla2025nt}, HyenaDNA \citep{nguyen2023hyenadna}, Enformer \citep{avsec2021enformer}, and PARM \citep{aerts2024parm}---are now the default backbone for \emph{cis}-regulatory prediction tasks. Their internal representations are commonly interpreted as encoding ``regulatory grammar'': the rules by which transcription factor (TF) binding sites combine, in particular orientations and spacings, to drive expression \citep{sample2019deep}. This interpretation has practical consequences: it is the basis for using model embeddings to design synthetic enhancers, predict variant effects, and rank putative regulatory elements.

We ask a sharper question: \emph{do these models actually encode arrangement of motifs, or only their identity?} A DNA foundation model satisfies the \textbf{billboard model} if, conditioned on the multiset of TF binding sites in a sequence, model output is approximately invariant to the positions, orientations, and spacings of those sites. Under this view, an enhancer is a billboard---what matters is which logos appear, not their layout.

Testing this is hard because of a confound: shuffling motif positions necessarily perturbs the spacer DNA between motifs, which itself carries strong compositional signal (GC content, $k$-mer frequencies, DNA shape). Naive grammar-sensitivity tests therefore inflate apparent grammar effects with spacer-composition effects. We address this directly.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{SF-GSI}, a Spacer-Factored Grammar Sensitivity Index that isolates arrangement effects from spacer composition through matched-spacer perturbations and a per-enhancer null distribution (Section~\ref{sec:method}).
  \item \textbf{GRAMLANG}, an evaluation suite of $7{,}650$ model--enhancer measurements spanning five MPRA datasets (K562 \citep{agarwal2025}, HepG2 \citep{klein2020}, neural \citep{inoue2019}, yeast \citep{vaishnav2022}, plant \citep{jores2021}) and five models, plus two positive controls (synthetic grammar, helical periodicity).
  \item A consistent finding across architectures and assays: $89.7\%$ ($95\%$ CI: $87.7$--$91.7\%$) of enhancers fit the billboard model. After spacer factoring and FDR, the grammar-sensitive fraction collapses from $100\%$ to $0.12\%$.
  \item Evidence that the billboard model is a \emph{model} property, not a biological one: $363$ MPRA pairs with identical motif vocabularies differ in expression by up to $\Delta = 6.3$ in $\log_2$-fold space, and engineered helical periodicity is undetectable in embeddings (Section~\ref{sec:completeness}).
\end{enumerate}

The implication is that current DNA foundation models internalize a TF-presence-and-abundance representation but not arrangement. This is a concrete, measurable capability gap that benchmarks should target and architectures should address.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{DNA foundation models.}
DNABERT \citep{ji2021dnabert} and DNABERT-2 \citep{zhou2023dnabert2} apply BERT-style masked language modeling to genomic sequence; the Nucleotide Transformer family \citep{dalla2025nt} scales this to multi-species pretraining; HyenaDNA \citep{nguyen2023hyenadna} replaces attention with implicit long convolutions to reach megabase context. Enformer \citep{avsec2021enformer} and PARM \citep{aerts2024parm} are supervised models on accessibility and MPRA data respectively. Standard evaluation focuses on prediction accuracy on held-out genomic data; mechanistic probes are rarer.

\paragraph{Mechanistic probes of regulatory grammar.}
Synthetic-sequence MPRA studies have shown that motif arrangement, orientation, and spacing affect expression \citep{smith2013syntax,king2020synthetic,georgakopoulos2023}. Probing analyses of foundation-model representations have largely focused on motif \emph{identity} recovery via attention or attribution \citep{ji2021dnabert,zhou2023dnabert2}; the few that examine grammar do so without spacer controls, leaving the apparent effect confounded with composition. Concurrent work by \citet{cheng2026mit} introduces a mechanistic invariance test for genomic language models and reaches a compatible conclusion---that current models fail to encode positional regulatory logic---using a different perturbation family; we view their result as orthogonal evidence for the billboard model and discuss the connection in Section~\ref{sec:discussion}.

\paragraph{Object-level vs.\ bag-of-words representations.}
The billboard model is a regulatory analogue of the bag-of-words limitation in NLP \citep{harris1954distributional}: models that capture occurrence statistics without sequential structure. Our test is in spirit a NLI-style probe \citep{conneau2018senteval} for arrangement.

\section{Method: Spacer-Factored Grammar Sensitivity}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Definitions}

Let $x \in \{A,C,G,T\}^L$ be an enhancer sequence with motif occurrences at positions $\mathcal{M}(x) = \{(m_i, p_i, o_i)\}_{i=1}^{k}$, where $m_i$ is the motif identity (TF), $p_i$ is the position, and $o_i$ is the orientation. Let $f_\theta : \{A,C,G,T\}^L \to \mathbb{R}$ denote a foundation-model expression probe (a linear/MLP head fit on top of frozen embeddings; see Section~\ref{sec:setup}).

A \emph{vocabulary-preserving perturbation} $\pi$ produces a sequence $x' = \pi(x)$ with $\{m_i'\} = \{m_i\}$ as multisets, but with arrangement $\{(p_i', o_i')\}$ resampled. Three perturbation operators are used:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Motif-only ($\pi_{\mathrm{mo}}$):} reorder motif tokens at fixed scaffold positions, preserving spacer DNA byte-for-byte.
  \item \textbf{Matched-spacer ($\pi_{\mathrm{ms}}$):} reorder motifs and resample spacer DNA from the empirical dinucleotide model of $x$, holding GC content and dinucleotide frequencies of spacers fixed.
  \item \textbf{Free shuffle ($\pi_{\mathrm{fs}}$):} reorder motifs and replace spacers with i.i.d.\ samples from a uniform background.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Naive GSI and its failure mode}

The naive Grammar Sensitivity Index is
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{GSI}(x) = \frac{\mathrm{Var}_{\pi}\!\left[f_\theta(\pi(x))\right]}{\mathrm{Var}\!\left[f_\theta(x_{\mathrm{null}})\right]},
\end{equation}
where the denominator is the variance of the model output across an unrelated control population. A factorial decomposition of $\mathrm{Var}_\pi$ (Figure~\ref{fig:spacer}A) shows that under any non-motif-only perturbation, the spacer-composition component dominates: in the K562 (Agarwal) data, spacer accounts for $62$--$78\%$ of perturbation variance; in HepG2 (Klein) and plant (Jores), $73$--$86\%$. Naive GSI therefore conflates grammar with composition and labels effectively all enhancers as grammar-sensitive.

\subsection{SF-GSI}

We define the \textbf{Spacer-Factored GSI} via the matched-spacer perturbation and a per-enhancer null:
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{SF\text{-}GSI}(x) =
  \frac{\mathrm{Var}\!\left[f_\theta(\pi_{\mathrm{ms}}(x))\right] - \mathrm{Var}\!\left[f_\theta(\pi_{\mathrm{ms}}^{(0)}(x))\right]}{\mathrm{Var}\!\left[f_\theta(\pi_{\mathrm{ms}}^{(0)}(x))\right]},
  \label{eq:sfgsi}
\end{equation}
where $\pi_{\mathrm{ms}}^{(0)}$ is a label-permuted null in which the same matched-spacer operator is applied but motif identities are randomized within their multiset class, breaking any arrangement signal while preserving spacer matching. The null is computed per enhancer with $N=30$ permutations, yielding a one-sided $p$-value and FDR $q$-value (Benjamini--Hochberg) per (enhancer, model, dataset) triple. An enhancer is \emph{grammar-sensitive} if $q < 0.05$ and $\mathrm{SF\text{-}GSI} > 0$. The complement defines billboard enhancers.

\subsection{Compositional regression baseline}

To upper-bound the variance attributable to non-arrangement features, we fit ridge regressions $\hat f(x) = \beta^\top \phi(x)$ from compositional features $\phi(x)$ alone (GC content, mono-/di-/trinucleotide frequencies, DNA-shape descriptors \citep{zhou2013dnashape}) to model output. The fraction of $f_\theta(x)$ variance explained by $\phi(x)$ alone is reported as $R^2_{\mathrm{comp}}$ (Figure~\ref{fig:spacer}C).

\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

\paragraph{Models.} We probe five foundation models: DNABERT-2 \citep{zhou2023dnabert2} ($768$-d), Nucleotide Transformer v2 \citep{dalla2025nt} ($1024$-d), HyenaDNA \citep{nguyen2023hyenadna} ($256$-d), Enformer \citep{avsec2021enformer}, and PARM \citep{aerts2024parm}. For Enformer and PARM we use the published heads; for the language models we train a linear ridge probe on frozen mean-pooled embeddings (Adam, weight decay $10^{-3}$, $5{,}000$ training sequences per dataset). Probe quality is reported in Appendix~\ref{app:probes}; we proceed only with viable probes ($\mathrm{Pearson}\,r > 0.3$ on held-out test data).

\paragraph{Datasets.} Five MPRA libraries spanning four organisms and five contexts: Agarwal et al.\ \citep{agarwal2025} K562 ($n=200{,}000$), Klein et al.\ \citep{klein2020} HepG2, Inoue et al.\ \citep{inoue2019} neural progenitors, Vaishnav et al.\ \citep{vaishnav2022} \emph{S.\ cerevisiae}, and Jores et al.\ \citep{jores2021} maize. Motifs are called with FIMO \citep{grant2011fimo} against JASPAR2024 \citep{rauluseviciute2024jaspar} ($p < 10^{-4}$). After filtering for $\geq 2$ motifs and length compatibility, $5{,}000$ enhancers per dataset are retained.

\paragraph{Compute and reproducibility.} All probes and analyses run on a single A100 GPU; total compute $<200$ GPU-hours. Code, environment specification, and a single-command pipeline (\texttt{scripts/run\_full\_pipeline.py}) are available at an anonymized repository.\footnote{Anonymized URL provided in supplementary material.}

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Spacer composition dominates apparent grammar effects}
\label{sec:spacer}

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{fig1_spacer_confound.pdf}
  \caption{\textbf{Absolute context dominates apparent grammar effects.} \textbf{(A)}~Factorial decomposition of prediction variance into position, orientation, and spacer components across three MPRA datasets (DNABERT-2); spacer composition consistently accounts for the largest share. \textbf{(B)}~Spacer ablation series: replacing spacer DNA (dinucleotide shuffle, GC shift, random replacement) produces larger expression changes than motif-only rearrangement. \textbf{(C)}~Feature-based regression $R^2$ predicting model expression output from compositional sequence features alone explains $40$--$80\%$ of variance. \textbf{(D)}~GC--expression correlation reverses sign across datasets, illustrating that models learn dataset-specific compositional biases rather than a universal positional grammar.}
  \label{fig:spacer}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:spacer}A decomposes prediction variance under vocabulary-preserving perturbations into orthogonal contributions from motif position, orientation, and spacer composition. Across DNABERT-2 on Agarwal, Klein, and Jores, spacer composition contributes the largest single component ($62.3\%$, $73.4\%$, $85.6\%$ respectively). A spacer-ablation series (Figure~\ref{fig:spacer}B) confirms causal direction: dinucleotide-shuffling spacers, while leaving motif arrangement untouched, perturbs the model output more than rearranging motifs while preserving spacers. Compositional features alone (GC, $k$-mers, DNA shape) explain $40$--$80\%$ of model-output variance (Figure~\ref{fig:spacer}C). The compositional bias is also non-universal: GC content correlates positively with expression in some datasets and negatively in others (Figure~\ref{fig:spacer}D), reflecting dataset-specific library design rather than transferable rules.

\subsection{Positive controls: models do not detect engineered grammar}
\label{sec:positive}

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{fig2_positive_control.pdf}
  \caption{\textbf{Positive controls---models fail to detect grammar that is biologically real.} \textbf{(A)}~Synthetic-grammar test: $150$ sequences with engineered ordering rules between two motifs (high-grammar vs.\ low-grammar vs.\ no-grammar classes) yield indistinguishable model predictions; $t$-test high-vs-low: $p=0.73$. \textbf{(B)}~Embedding centroid similarity across grammar classes is $0.997$. \textbf{(C)}~Helical periodicity scan: motif pairs at spacings $5$--$50$\,bp; the $10.5$-bp helical periodicity is undetectable, $F$-test $p=0.96$. \textbf{(D)}~Cohen's $d$ for arrangement contrasts is below $0.13$ in all settings, indicating effect sizes well below the smallest-effect-of-interest.}
  \label{fig:positive}
\end{figure}

If a model truly encoded arrangement, controlled in-silico experiments should reveal it. We construct two minimal positive controls (Figure~\ref{fig:positive}). \textbf{Synthetic grammar:} $150$ sequences with motifs $\mathtt{GATAAGAT}$ and $\mathtt{CACGTGAC}$ in fixed (high-grammar), reversed (low-grammar), or randomized (no-grammar) ordering. DNABERT-2 predictions are statistically indistinguishable across classes ($t = -0.34$, $p = 0.73$, Cohen's $d = -0.069$); embedding centroids are nearly identical (cosine similarity $0.997$). \textbf{Helical periodicity:} we vary spacings between two co-binding motifs from $5$ to $50$\,bp; the canonical $10.5$-bp $\mathrm{B}$-DNA periodicity is invisible to the model ($F$-test $p = 0.96$). Both controls have biological precedent for non-trivial effects on expression and genuine TF cooperativity. The model detects neither.

\subsection{Census: grammar-sensitive prevalence collapses under correction}
\label{sec:census}

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{fig3_gsi_census.pdf}
  \caption{\textbf{Statistical correction collapses apparent grammar prevalence.} \textbf{(A)}~Violin plots of raw GSI values by model; distributions are similar across DNABERT-2, NT, and HyenaDNA, indicating grammar sensitivity is not architecture-specific. \textbf{(B)}~Median GSI profiles across the five datasets; dataset identity drives more variation than model choice, consistent with the billboard view. \textbf{(C)}~Correction cascade: grammar-sensitive calls fall from $100\%$ under naive thresholding to $8.3\%$ after null normalization (SF-GSI) and to $0.12\%$ ($9/7{,}650$) after FDR correction. \textbf{(D)}~Pairwise cross-model GSI correlations by dataset; strong agreement on three datasets, near-zero or negative on Vaishnav and Inoue.}
  \label{fig:census}
\end{figure}

We compute SF-GSI for $7{,}650$ (model, dataset, enhancer) triples (Figure~\ref{fig:census}). Raw GSI values are similarly distributed across architectures (Figure~\ref{fig:census}A), and dataset identity drives more between-condition variation than model choice (Figure~\ref{fig:census}B). The headline result is the correction cascade in Figure~\ref{fig:census}C: $100\% \to 8.3\% \to 0.12\%$ as we move from naive thresholding, to null-normalized SF-GSI, to FDR. Bootstrapping over enhancers ($B = 10{,}000$) gives a billboard fraction of $89.7\%$ ($95\%$ CI: $87.7$--$91.7\%$). Per-dataset billboard fractions are $83.8\%$ (Agarwal), $94.6\%$ (Klein), and $90.7\%$ (Jores). Cross-model GSI correlations (Figure~\ref{fig:census}D) are strong on Agarwal/Klein/Jores but near zero on Vaishnav and Inoue, indicating that even residual grammar signal is dataset-idiosyncratic and not a transferable property of ``what foundation models know.''

\subsection{Compositionality: grammar features add no variance over motif vocabulary}
\label{sec:compositional}

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{fig4_compositionality.pdf}
  \caption{\textbf{Vocabulary explains nearly all explainable variance.} Hierarchical variance decomposition of model output into vocabulary (motif identities + counts), grammar (positions + orientations + spacings), and embedding-residual components, across three datasets (DNABERT-2). Adding grammar features on top of vocabulary raises $R^2$ by $\Delta \leq 0.005$ in all settings, while the embedding contribution beyond grammar features is large, indicating models learn rich representations dominated by non-grammar signal.}
  \label{fig:compositional}
\end{figure}

We hierarchically decompose model output variance using nested linear regressions (Figure~\ref{fig:compositional}). On Agarwal/Jores/Inoue, adding $\sim 145$ explicit grammar features (pairwise distances, orientations, periodicities) on top of $\sim 130$ vocabulary features yields a grammar increment of $\Delta R^2 \in [-0.046, -0.0005]$---i.e.\ no positive contribution beyond vocabulary. The embedding-residual increment, by contrast, is large ($\Delta R^2 = 0.16$--$0.42$), confirming that foundation-model embeddings carry substantial information that linear feature regressions do not capture, but that this information is \emph{not} encoded in any explicit grammar feature.

\subsection{Cross-dataset transfer of motif effects}
\label{sec:transfer}

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{fig5_transfer.pdf}
  \caption{\textbf{Motif-identity effects transfer; arrangement effects do not.} Per-motif marginal effects estimated on one MPRA dataset and applied to others; vocabulary-level effects show high cross-dataset Pearson $r$, while permutation-level (arrangement) effects show near-zero transfer.}
  \label{fig:transfer}
\end{figure}

If models learned arrangement rules, those rules should transfer across MPRA libraries; if they learned vocabulary effects, those would also transfer (and indeed motif identity is universal across cell types in many contexts). Figure~\ref{fig:transfer} confirms the asymmetry: motif-marginal effects estimated on one dataset predict another at $r \in [0.4, 0.7]$, but estimated arrangement effects transfer at $r \approx 0$. Whatever residual ``grammar'' the models retain is dataset-idiosyncratic.

\subsection{Completeness: biological grammar exists but is invisible to models}
\label{sec:completeness}

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{fig6_completeness.pdf}
  \caption{\textbf{Biological grammar exists in MPRA data but is invisible to models.} \textbf{(A)}~Distribution of measured expression differences for $363$ sequence pairs with identical motif vocabularies but different arrangements, across Agarwal/Klein/Jores; $40\%$ of Agarwal pairs show $|\Delta| > 1.0$ in $\log_2$-fold space. \textbf{(B)}~Top pair: $\Delta = 6.3$ (yeast Vaishnav). \textbf{(C)}~Model predictions on the same pairs are essentially flat ($r = 0.04$ between predicted and measured $\Delta$).}
  \label{fig:completeness}
\end{figure}

The findings above could in principle be reconciled with the billboard model being biologically true. We show this is not the case. From Agarwal/Klein/Jores we extract $363$ sequence pairs that share motif vocabulary (same multiset of TF binding sites) but differ in arrangement. Measured expression differs in $40\%$ of pairs by $|\Delta| > 1$ and in the most extreme case by $\Delta = 6.3$ in $\log_2$-fold space (Figure~\ref{fig:completeness}). Foundation-model predictions on the same pairs show negligible correlation with the measured difference ($r = 0.04$). Combined with the helical-periodicity null (Section~\ref{sec:positive}), this establishes that the billboard property is a model property: grammar is biologically real but lies outside the representation hypothesis class.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

Our results converge on a simple statement: contemporary DNA foundation models behave, on \emph{cis}-regulatory tasks, as if they were trained to recognize and weight motif vocabulary, but not arrangement. Apparent grammar effects in naive probes are largely composition effects in disguise, and even rich training signal in MPRAs has not induced models to encode the spatial logic that biologists know is present.

\paragraph{Relation to concurrent work.} \citet{cheng2026mit} report that genomic language models fail a mechanistic invariance test, an independent diagnostic constructed from sequence transformations that should leave regulatory logic invariant. Their findings agree qualitatively with ours: position-aware regulatory rules are not represented in current foundation-model embeddings. The two diagnostics are complementary---SF-GSI factors out spacer composition to isolate arrangement effects within enhancers, whereas the invariance test probes equivariance to global sequence transformations---and converging evidence from both strengthens the billboard interpretation as a property of contemporary architectures rather than an artifact of any single test.

\paragraph{Why might this happen?} Three plausible mechanisms: \textbf{(1)}~the pretraining objective (masked-token reconstruction over the genome) is dominated by single-nucleotide and short $k$-mer statistics, with weak gradient on long-range arrangement; \textbf{(2)}~tokenization (BPE-style for DNABERT-2) destroys precise positional alignment between motifs; \textbf{(3)}~the supervision in MPRA fine-tuning is short-context expression, with high-variance labels that swamp small arrangement effects. We do not adjudicate among these here; we provide the diagnostic that lets future architectures be evaluated on the right axis.

\paragraph{What an arrangement-aware model would look like.} It would (i) detect the helical periodicity scan, (ii) discriminate engineered grammar classes, (iii) yield SF-GSI distributions whose post-FDR positive fraction substantially exceeds $0.12\%$, and (iv) predict the sign of expression differences in matched-vocabulary MPRA pairs at $r \gg 0.04$. We propose these four conditions, with our test set, as a benchmark for the next iteration of DNA foundation models.

\paragraph{Limitations.} (i) We probe frozen embeddings with linear/MLP heads; an arrangement signal that is present in embeddings but only readable by a deep nonlinear head would be missed---though our positive controls (Section~\ref{sec:positive}) suggest the signal is genuinely absent rather than merely hidden. (ii) Our motif calls rely on FIMO and JASPAR; alternative motif sets may shift quantitative estimates. (iii) The five MPRAs cover four cell types and two kingdoms but underrepresent developmental and tissue-specific contexts.

\paragraph{Broader impact.} Honest characterization of foundation-model capabilities is a prerequisite for safe deployment in biological design. Overestimating regulatory-grammar competence risks misleading downstream uses (synthetic enhancer design, variant interpretation, therapeutic target prioritization). Our diagnostic is conservative---it errs toward calling models grammar-insensitive---and we believe this is the appropriate failure mode in scientific tooling.

\section{Probe quality}
\label{app:probes}
Across $15$ (model, dataset) pairs we observe held-out Pearson $r$ from $0.17$ (HyenaDNA on Agarwal) to $0.58$ (DNABERT-2 on Jores). Pairs with $r < 0.3$ are flagged non-viable and excluded from the headline census; sensitivity analysis including all probes is given in Appendix~\ref{app:sensitivity}.

\section{Permutation null details}
\label{app:null}
Per enhancer, we draw $N=30$ matched-spacer permutations, recomputing model output for each. Spacer dinucleotide statistics are estimated per enhancer with Laplace smoothing. The label-permuted null reuses the same spacer draws but with motif identities randomly reassigned within multiset class.

\section{Sensitivity analyses}
\label{app:sensitivity}
We repeat the headline census (i)~including non-viable probes, (ii)~with $N \in \{10, 30, 100\}$ permutations, (iii)~under JASPAR2022 vs.\ JASPAR2024 motif libraries, (iv)~at $p$-value thresholds of $10^{-3}$ and $10^{-5}$ for FIMO calls. The post-FDR billboard fraction lies in $[88.4\%, 92.1\%]$ across all settings.

A100 ($40$\,GB) GPU; PyTorch $\geq 2.1$; total wall-clock $\sim 2$ days for the full pipeline.
