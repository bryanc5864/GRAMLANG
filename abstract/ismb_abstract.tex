\begin{center}
{\Large \textbf{Spacer composition, not motif grammar, drives regulatory signals in DNA sequence models}}
\end{center}

\section*{Background}

Whether enhancer activity is governed by a regulatory ``grammar'' of motif arrangement or by a more permissive billboard logic remains a central question in cis-regulatory biology~[1,2].
DNA foundation models now make it easy to test grammar \textit{in silico}, but standard vocabulary-preserving shuffle assays perturb both motif arrangement and the spacer DNA between motifs.
That creates an identification problem: observed prediction changes may reflect background sequence composition rather than true motif grammar.
We address this with a spacer-factored grammar sensitivity framework (SF-GSI) that explicitly separates arrangement effects from spacer-composition effects and asks which signal survives appropriate statistical control.

\section*{Methods}

SF-GSI starts from the grammar sensitivity index, $GSI = \sigma(\hat{y}) / |\mu(\hat{y})|$, computed over vocabulary-preserving motif shuffles, and then factors that response into motif position/orientation, spacer composition, and residual context effects using matched-spacer perturbations and null-normalized testing.
To our knowledge, this is the first framework to factorially decompose apparent grammar sensitivity into arrangement and composition components in this setting.
We applied SF-GSI to five MPRA datasets spanning human, yeast, and plant systems (Agarwal, Klein, Inoue, Vaishnav, and Jores), totaling 7,650 model--enhancer measurements: 3 foundation models $\times$ 5 datasets $\times$ 500 enhancers, plus Enformer on 3 human datasets $\times$ 50 enhancers.
The model set spans transformer, state-space, and MPRA-native families, including DNABERT-2, Nucleotide Transformer v2, HyenaDNA, Enformer, and PARM~[4--7].
Code and analysis pipelines are available at \url{https://github.com/bryanc5864/GRAMLANG}.

\section*{Results}

The dominant result is the correction cascade in Fig.~\ref{fig:census}: under naive thresholding, all tested enhancers appear grammar-sensitive, but this falls to 8.3\% after null normalization and to 0.17\% (13/7,650) after FDR correction.
That three-order-of-magnitude drop shows that widespread detectability under unconstrained shuffle assays is largely an artifact of the assay itself rather than evidence for pervasive motif-order logic.

SF-GSI explains why that collapse occurs.
Across datasets, spacer DNA composition accounts for 78--86\% of measured grammar-sensitivity variance, while simple compositional features explain 40--80\% of expression-prediction variance.
Motif-only rearrangements produce substantially smaller perturbation effects than spacer replacement or GC-shifting perturbations.
Arrangement-dependent signal is therefore present in the models, but it is dominated by nuisance variation introduced when spacer sequence is not controlled.

The residual signal that remains after correction is structured rather than random.
Model medians are narrowly separated, and cross-model agreement is concentrated in specific biological contexts rather than in a single architecture family.
Dataset context therefore matters more than nominal model class when asking where credible grammar-sensitive candidates are enriched.

Positive controls confirm that the framework does not erase genuine grammar.
When spacer sequence is held fixed in synthetic constructs, orientation effects remain strongly detectable across models ($p \le 7.2\times10^{-15}$, with two models reaching numerical underflow).
By contrast, cross-species transfer is near zero and variance decompositions assign only 0--1.6\% of explained variance to arrangement, versus 8--22\% to motif vocabulary.
Motif arrangement therefore contributes real but limited and context-specific signal, whereas unconstrained shuffles overwhelmingly measure spacer composition.

This matters beyond MPRA grammar analysis alone.
If benchmark perturbations fail to control nuisance sequence variables, they can systematically misstate what regulatory foundation models have learned.
SF-GSI therefore provides a stronger benchmark for mechanistic model evaluation.
Our measurements are based on MPRA activity rather than endogenous chromatin context, but that limitation strengthens rather than weakens the main methodological point: background-sequence control must be treated as a first-class design constraint in any computational study of cis-regulatory grammar.

\section*{Conclusions}

Spacer composition, not motif arrangement, explains most of the apparent regulatory grammar signal reported by standard shuffle-based assays.
Once null normalization and multiplicity correction are applied, only a small residual set of candidates remains, while matched-spacer positive controls confirm that genuine grammar can still be recovered.
For sequence-model benchmarking and enhancer biology alike, the practical implication is direct: claims about regulatory grammar should be made only under perturbation schemes that explicitly control spacer sequence.
