\begin{center}
{\Large \textbf{Spacer composition, not motif grammar, drives regulatory signals in DNA sequence models}}
\end{center}

\section*{Background}

Whether enhancer activity is governed by motif arrangement or by a more permissive billboard logic remains a central question in cis-regulatory biology~[1,2].
DNA foundation models make grammar perturbation easy \textit{in silico}, but standard vocabulary-preserving shuffles perturb both motif order and spacer DNA.
We address that identification problem with a spacer-factored grammar sensitivity framework (SF-GSI) that separates arrangement effects from spacer-composition effects and tests which signal survives statistical control.

\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.7\textwidth]{fig1_spacer_confound.pdf}
\caption{\textbf{Spacer composition dominates apparent grammar effects.} Spacer sequence explains most measured grammar sensitivity and induces larger prediction changes than motif rearrangement alone.}
\label{fig:spacer}
\end{figure}

\section*{Methods}

SF-GSI starts from the grammar sensitivity index, $GSI = \sigma(\hat{y}) / |\mu(\hat{y})|$, computed over vocabulary-preserving motif shuffles, and factors that response into motif position/orientation, spacer composition, and residual context effects using matched-spacer perturbations and null-normalized testing.
To our knowledge, this is the first framework to factorially decompose apparent grammar sensitivity into arrangement and composition components in this setting.
We applied SF-GSI to five MPRA datasets (Agarwal, Klein, Inoue, Vaishnav, and Jores), totaling 7,650 model--enhancer measurements: 3 foundation models $\times$ 5 datasets $\times$ 500 enhancers, plus Enformer on 3 human datasets $\times$ 50 enhancers.
The model set spans transformer, state-space, and MPRA-native families, including DNABERT-2, Nucleotide Transformer v2, HyenaDNA, Enformer, and PARM~[4--7]. Code: \url{https://github.com/bryanc5864/GRAMLANG}.
\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.7\textwidth]{fig3_gsi_census.pdf}
\caption{\textbf{Statistical correction collapses apparent grammar prevalence.} Calls fall from 100\% under naive thresholding to 8.3\% after null normalization and to 0.17\% (13/7,650) after FDR correction.}
\label{fig:census}
\end{figure}

\section*{Results}

The dominant result is the correction cascade in Fig.~\ref{fig:census}: under naive thresholding, all tested enhancers appear grammar-sensitive, but this falls to 8.3\% after null normalization and to 0.17\% (13/7,650) after FDR correction.
That three-order-of-magnitude drop shows that widespread detectability under unconstrained shuffle assays is largely an artifact of the assay itself rather than evidence for pervasive motif-order logic.

SF-GSI explains why that collapse occurs.
As shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:spacer}, spacer DNA composition accounts for 78--86\% of measured grammar-sensitivity variance, while compositional features explain 40--80\% of expression-prediction variance.
Motif-only rearrangements produce substantially smaller perturbation effects than spacer replacement or GC-shifting perturbations, showing that arrangement-dependent signal is present but dominated by nuisance variation when spacer sequence is not controlled.

The residual signal after correction is structured rather than random.
Cross-model agreement is concentrated in specific biological contexts, indicating that dataset context matters more than nominal architecture when asking where credible grammar-sensitive candidates are enriched.
Positive controls confirm that the framework does not erase genuine grammar: when spacer sequence is held fixed in synthetic constructs, orientation effects remain strongly detectable across models ($p \le 7.2\times10^{-15}$, with two models reaching numerical underflow).
By contrast, cross-species transfer is near zero and variance decompositions assign only 0--1.6\% of explained variance to arrangement, versus 8--22\% to motif vocabulary.
These findings matter beyond MPRA grammar analysis because benchmark perturbations that fail to control nuisance sequence variables can systematically misstate what regulatory foundation models have learned.

\section*{Conclusions}

Spacer composition, not motif arrangement, explains most of the apparent regulatory grammar signal reported by standard shuffle-based assays.
Matched-spacer positive controls still recover genuine grammar, but claims about regulatory grammar should be made only under perturbation schemes that explicitly control spacer sequence.
