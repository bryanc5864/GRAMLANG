\begin{center}
{\large \textbf{Spacer composition dominates apparent regulatory grammar
signals detected by DNA sequence models}}
\vspace{3pt}
\end{center}
\vspace{1pt}
\hrule
\vspace{3pt}

\section*{Motivation}

Whether the spatial arrangement of transcription factor (TF) binding sites encodes functional information---a ``regulatory grammar''---or whether enhancers operate as ``billboards'' where only TF identity matters remains debated~[1,2].
DNA sequence models enable systematic \textit{in silico} perturbation of enhancer architecture, but standard grammar sensitivity metrics may confound motif arrangement with spacer DNA composition.
We introduce a \textbf{spacer-factored grammar sensitivity framework (SF-GSI)} to separate these effects across four model architectures, five MPRA datasets, and four organisms.

\vspace{-4pt}
\section*{Methods}

\textbf{Grammar Sensitivity Index (GSI).}
TF motifs are identified via FIMO against JASPAR 2024~[3]; vocabulary-preserving shuffles permute motif order while holding motif identity constant.
GSI~$= \sigma(\hat{y}) / |\mu(\hat{y})|$.
\textbf{Spacer-Factored GSI (SF-GSI).}
A factorial design isolates: (i)~motif position/orientation (true grammar), (ii)~spacer composition (confound), (iii)~flanking-context effects via matched-spacer shuffles and regression residuals.
\textbf{Rule Extraction.}
Pairwise rules via spacing scans (2--50~bp), four orientation configurations, and helical phasing at ${\sim}$10.5~bp.
\textbf{Variance Decomposition.}
Hierarchical ANOVA: vocabulary $\rightarrow$ +grammar $\rightarrow$ +higher-order $\rightarrow$ full model.
\textbf{Models \& Data.}
DNABERT-2, Nucleotide Transformer v2-500M, HyenaDNA, Enformer, and MPRA-trained PARM; five MPRA datasets across human, yeast, and plant with 7{,}650 model--enhancer measurements in the main census plus controlled synthetic positive controls~[4,5].

\vspace{-2pt}
\section*{Results}

\textbf{The spacer confound.}
Across datasets, spacer DNA composition accounts for 78--86\% of measured GSI variance (Fig.~\ref{fig:spacer}A--B). Simple compositional statistics explain 40--80\% of model predictions (Fig.~\ref{fig:spacer}C), and the GC--expression relationship reverses between species (Fig.~\ref{fig:spacer}D).

\vspace{-4pt}
\textbf{Correction cascade.}
In the 7{,}650-measurement census, naive thresholding marks all enhancers as grammar-sensitive, but null-normalized testing reduces this to 8.3\%, and FDR correction to \textbf{0.17\%} (13/7{,}550)---a three-order-of-magnitude drop consistent across architectures (Fig.~\ref{fig:census}C--D).

\textbf{Positive controls.}
When spacer sequence is held constant in controlled synthetic constructs, orientation effects remain detectable across models ($p \le 7.2\times10^{-15}$; two models reach numerical underflow), confirming that the framework can recover genuine grammar effects when the confound is removed.

\vspace{-4pt}
\textbf{Transfer \& variance decomposition.}
Grammar rules from one organism predict near-zero effects in others (Fig.~\ref{fig:transfer}A). In ANOVA decompositions, motif vocabulary explains 8--22\% of variance, whereas arrangement contributes only 0--1.6\%. Learned embeddings outperform explicit grammar features by 16--42 percentage points of $R^2$, indicating that current motif-centric grammar summaries miss most predictive signal (Fig.~\ref{fig:transfer}B).

\section*{Conclusion}

Across these datasets and models, most apparent regulatory grammar signal arises from spacer composition rather than motif arrangement. Genuine grammar effects are still detectable in spacer-controlled positive controls, but standard vocabulary-preserving shuffles substantially overestimate their prevalence.
SF-GSI provides a practical route to separate grammatical effects from compositional confounds in regulatory genomics and offers a stronger benchmark for evaluating sequence models as mechanistic predictors.
