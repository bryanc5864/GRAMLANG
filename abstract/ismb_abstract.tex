\section*{Motivation}

Whether the spatial arrangement of transcription factor (TF) binding sites encodes functional information---a ``regulatory grammar''---or whether enhancers operate as ``billboards'' where only TF identity matters remains debated~[1,2].
DNA foundation models enable systematic \textit{in silico} perturbation of enhancer architecture, but standard grammar sensitivity metrics confound motif arrangement with spacer DNA composition.
We introduce a \textbf{spacer-factored grammar sensitivity framework (SF-GSI)} applied across four foundation models, five MPRA datasets, and four organisms to quantify the true contribution of motif arrangement to gene expression.

\vspace{-4pt}
\section*{Methods}

\textbf{Grammar Sensitivity Index (GSI).}
TF motifs are identified via FIMO against JASPAR 2024~[3]; 1{,}000 vocabulary-preserving shuffles permute motif order while holding identity constant.
GSI~$= \sigma(\hat{y}) / |\mu(\hat{y})|$.
\textbf{Spacer-Factored GSI (SF-GSI).}
A factorial design isolates: (i)~motif position/orientation (true grammar), (ii)~spacer composition (confound), (iii)~flanking-context effects via matched-spacer shuffles and regression residuals.
\textbf{Rule Extraction.}
Pairwise rules via spacing scans (2--50~bp), four orientation configurations, and helical phasing at ${\sim}$10.5~bp.
\textbf{Variance Decomposition.}
Hierarchical ANOVA: vocabulary $\rightarrow$ +grammar $\rightarrow$ +higher-order $\rightarrow$ full model.
\textbf{Models \& Data.}
DNABERT-2, Nucleotide Transformer v2-500M (transformers), HyenaDNA (SSM), PARM (CNN); five MPRA datasets across human, yeast, and plant (${\sim}$7{,}650 enhancers)~[4,5].

\vspace{-2pt}
\section*{Results}

\textbf{The spacer confound.}
Spacer DNA composition accounts for 78--86\% of measured GSI variance (Fig.~\ref{fig:spacer}A--B). Simple compositional statistics explain 40--80\% of predictions (Fig.~\ref{fig:spacer}C), and the GC--expression relationship reverses between species (Fig.~\ref{fig:spacer}D).

\vspace{-4pt}
\textbf{Correction cascade.}
Naive testing yields 100\% ``grammatical'' enhancers; $z$-score normalization reduces this to 8.3\%, and FDR correction to \textbf{0.17\%}---a three-order-of-magnitude drop consistent across all architectures (Fig.~\ref{fig:census}C--D).

\textbf{Positive controls.}
With spacer composition held constant, orientation effects are detected at $p < 10^{-117}$ with 100\% detection rate (vs.\ 8.3\% under standard shuffles), confirming methodological sensitivity; the null result reflects biology.

\textbf{Variance decomposition.}
Motif identity explains 8--22\% of expression variance; grammar adds only 0--1.6\% $R^2$. Replicate ceilings (${\sim}$85\% $R^2$) indicate most variance is attributable to \textit{trans}-regulatory factors beyond enhancer sequence.

\textbf{Cross-species transfer.}
Grammar rules from one organism predict near-zero effects in others (Fig.~\ref{fig:transfer}), consistent with the absence of conserved regulatory syntax.

\section*{Conclusion}

Regulatory DNA operates predominantly as a \textit{billboard}: TF identity, not arrangement, determines enhancer activity. Apparent grammar is largely spacer composition---a confound inflating sensitivity ${\sim}$1{,}000$\times$. Findings hold across four architectures, five datasets, and four organisms.
SF-GSI provides the statistical machinery to separate grammatical effects from compositional confounds in regulatory genomics.
